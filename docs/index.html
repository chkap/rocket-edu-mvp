The Tsiolkovsky rocket equation

Plain-text form: Δv = ve ln(m0/mf)

Explanation

The Tsiolkovsky rocket equation tells you how much change in velocity a rocket can gain (called Δv, pronounced “delta-v”) from burning its propellant. Think of Δv as the rocket’s “speed budget” for a maneuver.

This equation is an idealized physics model: it assumes the engine’s performance stays the same during the burn and it ignores losses like air drag and gravity pulling you down while you burn. Real missions have those extra losses, but the rocket equation is still the starting point for understanding what’s possible.

Variables (what the symbols mean)

Δv (delta-v)
The change in velocity the rocket can produce by burning fuel, measured in meters per second (m/s). Bigger Δv means the rocket can speed up (or slow down) more.
Isp (specific impulse)
A measure of how efficient an engine is, measured in seconds (s). Higher Isp means the engine gets more “push” out of the same amount of propellant.
g0 (standard gravity)
A constant used to connect Isp to exhaust speed: g0 = 9.80665 m/s². It’s based on Earth’s gravity at sea level.
m0 (wet mass)
The rocket’s mass before the burn, fully fueled (in kilograms, kg).
mf (dry mass)
The rocket’s mass after the burn, with the propellant used up (in kilograms, kg). This is sometimes called “dry mass” or “empty mass.”

Many calculators use an equivalent form of the equation: Δv = Isp × g0 × ln(m0/mf). Here, ln means the natural logarithm, and the fraction m0/mf is the mass ratio (how many times heavier the rocket is when full compared to when empty).

Worked example

Falcon 9 Stage 1 (Block 5) — plug in real numbers

Inputs

Isp ≈ 282 s (sea level)

m0 ≈ 433,100 kg (wet mass)

mf ≈ 25,600 kg (dry mass)

Result

Δv = Isp × 9.80665 × ln(m0/mf)

Δv ≈ 7,822 m/s (7.82 km/s)

(This is the ideal value from the equation for these inputs.)

Show the step-by-step calculation
  1. Mass ratio: m0/mf = 433100 / 25600 ≈ 16.914
  2. Natural log: ln(m0/mf) ≈ ln(16.914) ≈ 2.828
  3. Plug into the equation: Δv ≈ 282 × 9.80665 × 2.828 ≈ 7822 m/s

What does ~7.8 km/s mean? Δv is a rocket’s “speed budget” in an ideal world. A value around 7–8 km/s is in the same ballpark as the speed needed for low Earth orbit (about 7.8 km/s), but real launches need more than orbital speed because gravity and air drag steal some of that budget while you climb.

That’s why Falcon 9 needs a second stage: Stage 1 provides a huge push early on, then Stage 2 continues accelerating above most of the atmosphere to reach orbit.

(You should see ~7,822 m/s.)

Intuition

The tricky part of rockets is that fuel has to lift (and accelerate) other fuel. Early in the burn, you’re heavy, so each second of thrust has to speed up a lot of mass. Later in the burn, you’re lighter, so the same engine can change your velocity faster. This “snowball” effect is why adding more fuel helps, but with diminishing returns.

The natural log (ln) captures those diminishing returns. If Δv is proportional to ln(m0/mf), then to get double the Δv you need square the mass ratio: doubling Δv means doubling the log, and doubling a log means squaring what’s inside. That’s why rockets often use staging (dropping empty tanks/engines to reduce mass) — it helps reset the mass ratio — but staging is out of scope for this simple calculator.
